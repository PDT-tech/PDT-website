Barbershop harmony in the American tradition

About barbershop

Four parts. No instruments. Pure harmony.

Barbershop harmony is a style of a cappella singing in four parts — tenor, lead, baritone, and bass — where all four voices lock into rich, resonant chords that seem to create overtones beyond what any single voice can produce alone.

It's a uniquely American art form, rooted in the informal singing that took place in barbershops and town squares in the late 19th century. Today it's practiced by hundreds of thousands of singers worldwide.

At its best, barbershop produces what singers call "the ring" — a sparkling acoustic phenomenon that happens when four voices are perfectly in tune and in balance. Once you've heard it, you never forget it.

The voice parts

Tenor · Lead · Baritone · Bass

Tenor
The high harmony
Sings above the melody, adding brightness and sparkle to the chord.
Lead
The melody
Carries the tune — the voice the audience follows.
Baritone
The harmonic glue
Fills in the chord, sometimes above and sometimes below the lead.
Bass
The foundation
Anchors the chord from below, giving the harmony its depth and resonance.

Repertoire

What we sing

Our song list is built for community performance — singable, familiar, joyful, and suited to audiences of all ages. We draw from classic barbershop literature, American standards, popular songs of the 20th century, and seasonal favorites.

Every song is arranged for four-part close harmony. Our Music Director selects and arranges material that showcases the chorus's strengths while keeping performances fresh and engaging.
